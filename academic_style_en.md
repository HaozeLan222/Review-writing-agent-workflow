# English Academic Style Guide

This file is used during the revision stage of English literature review drafts. It focuses on clarity, academic tone, citation preservation, and review-appropriate structure. It must not introduce new facts, data, papers, examples, or conclusions.

## Use Cases

- Polishing English literature review sections.
- Revising abstracts, introductions, related work sections, and conclusions.
- Improving paragraph flow and argumentative clarity.
- Checking whether claims, citation anchors, and terminology are consistent.

## Core Principles

- Preserve the original facts, claims, citations, and structure.
- Do not add new papers, datasets, metrics, results, or author opinions.
- Do not remove necessary citation anchors such as `[P01]`.
- Do not turn tentative findings into definitive claims.
- Prioritize academic accuracy over fluency.

## Style Rules

### 1. Academic Tone

Use neutral, evidence-based academic language.

Prefer:

```text
The study reports that...
The authors evaluate...
The results suggest...
This limitation indicates...
```

Avoid unsupported promotional phrasing:

```text
This revolutionary method completely solves...
This groundbreaking framework proves...
```

### 2. Claim Strength

Match the strength of the wording to the evidence.

- Use `shows`, `demonstrates`, or `achieves` only when the paper provides direct evidence.
- Use `suggests`, `indicates`, or `may imply` when the conclusion is inferential.
- Use `claims` or `argues` when reporting an author's position rather than an independently verified result.

### 3. Literature Review Structure

A strong English review paragraph usually contains:

1. A topic sentence that states the dimension of comparison.
2. Evidence from one or more papers.
3. Synthesis or contrast across papers.
4. A short transition to the next issue.

Avoid writing one isolated summary paragraph per paper unless the section explicitly requires annotated bibliography style.

### 4. Citation Preservation

Keep citation anchors attached to the factual claims they support. If a sentence is split, move the citation anchor to the sentence that still carries the evidence.

Example:

```text
Original: The method improves search efficiency and uses evaluator feedback to guide generation [P03].
Revised: The method uses evaluator feedback to guide generation [P03]. This feedback mechanism is reported to improve search efficiency in the evaluated setting [P03].
```

### 5. Tense

Common conventions:

- Use present tense for established knowledge or paper contributions: `The paper proposes...`
- Use past tense for specific experiments: `The authors evaluated the method on...`
- Use present perfect for research trends: `Recent studies have explored...`

### 6. Terminology

Define important abbreviations on first use:

```text
large language models (LLMs)
reinforcement learning (RL)
combinatorial optimization (CO)
```

After definition, use the abbreviation consistently.

## Output Requirement

When asked to polish or revise a passage, output only the revised text unless the user explicitly asks for explanations, alternatives, or comments.
